History & Philosophy

What is Freemasonry?

An introduction to the tenets, structures, and history of the fraternity.

Freemasonry is mankind’s oldest and largest fraternity. Found throughout the free world, it is a system of morality and character development focused on tradition, ritual, education, charity, and fellowship. Within the wider "Family of Freemasonry" are opportunities for men, women, and youth to participate in social, intellectual, and community service activities.

The fraternity's rich symbolism is derived from the "operative" guilds of stone masons who built the castles and cathedrals of medieval Europe. Influenced by the Enlightenment, Freemasonry evolved from a guild of physical builders into a philosophical society of "speculative" builders, using the tools of the mason—like the square, compasses, and plumb line—as symbols to teach lessons on integrity, moderation, and brotherly love.

A Society with Secrets, Not a Secret Society

A common misconception is that Freemasonry is a secret society. In truth, it is a society with secrets, operating in plain sight. Its meeting halls are public, its members are known, and its constitutions are published.

The "mysteries" of Freemasonry are not about hidden treasures or conspiracies of power. Rather, they consist of modes of recognition—grips, signs, and words—that allow brothers to recognize and trust one another across borders, languages, and cultures, ensuring a traveler can find a home and a helping hand wherever he goes.

Lodges, Degrees, and Rites

At its core, Freemasonry consists of three progressive degrees of initiation:

  • Entered Apprentice: Symbolizing youth and the beginning of a member's moral and intellectual development.
  • Fellow Craft: Representing manhood, science, and the study of the liberal arts.
  • Master Mason: Representing maturity and the culmination of masonic training. Once attained, a Master Mason is equal in standing to all other Masons, regardless of rank or station.

These degrees are conferred in the basic unit of Freemasonry—the local "Blue Lodge." Beyond the Blue Lodge, Master Masons may join appendant bodies such as the Scottish Rite or York Rite for further philosophical study.

Our German Masonic Heritage

The 9th Manhattan District occupies a unique place in New York Masonry. Historically designated as the German District, it was founded to serve the waves of German immigrants who arrived in New York in the 19th century.

To help these immigrants integrate while preserving their native tongue and cultural camaraderie, the Grand Lodge of New York authorized lodges to work in the German language. These lodges became vital centers of community life, providing charitable aid and establishing institutions like the German Masonic Charitable Foundation (GMCF). Today, the district honors this rich history by maintaining the bonds of charity, community, and brotherhood that have defined us for generations.
